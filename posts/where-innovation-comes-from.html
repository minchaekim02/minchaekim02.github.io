Where Innovation Comes From: Reflections on Jensen Huang’s Talk

“Innovation comes from a firm belief that something will happen five to ten years from now—that this technology will be needed.”

I recently attended NVIDIA’s Build-a-Claw special lecture. With the release of OpenClaw, NVIDIA’s DGX Spark and Apple’s Mac mini have drawn renewed attention as personal computing environments for the next generation. Unlike the question-and-answer style in which I had mostly used LLMs, OpenClaw turns the interaction into a longer-running assistant: it receives instructions through channels such as Telegram or Slack and, within the tools and permissions it is given, carries out multi-step tasks. Being open source, it can be configured with considerable flexibility—though for a local, always-on setup, it effectively requires a personal machine that stays running at all times. This is why NVIDIA’s DGX Spark and Apple’s Mac mini have drawn renewed attention among developers as possible homes for such always-on local agents.

Looking at Technology Through the Lens of the Future

What left the strongest impression was Jensen Huang’s way of looking at technology—not measuring it against present demand, but against a future that had not yet arrived.

When personal computing was still being defined, Huang imagined what would come next and helped build technologies before the market had fully formed. The early DGX systems carried a similar spirit: NVIDIA built DGX-1 specifically for deep learning in 2016, and the first system reached OpenAI at a moment when the future scale of AI was still difficult to foresee. What followed became the backbone of today’s AI research infrastructure.

The infrastructure NVIDIA built has since become a foundation the entire AI ecosystem relies on—from CPUs and GPUs to AI agents and Physical AI.

As he put it:

“Innovation comes from a firm belief that something will happen five to ten years from now—that this technology will be needed.”

And when an early attempt did not succeed, he did not give up—he immediately set about building something new and moved toward the next possibility.

Apple, Microsoft, Tesla, Meta—many of the companies that reshaped the world were built in exactly this way. Rather than responding to existing demand, they imagined changes most people had yet to sense and prepared for a future they believed in.

I, too, want to become someone who looks at the future that way—sensing problems before they come clearly into view, and preparing the research to address them.

Build-a-Claw, and the Promise of AI Agents

The Build-a-Claw demo, which had been the day’s main purpose, took place after Jensen Huang’s talk. We connected a DGX Spark to a computer and a phone, and as commands were issued through Telegram, we could watch several agents being orchestrated in real time. Going beyond answering a single question, watching the agents distribute and carry out tasks on their own was clearly a different experience from how I had used LLMs before.

If an agent were to act on files stored on a personal machine, concerns about privacy and security would naturally arise. At the same time, it opened up an imagination of computing unlike anything before. And the more deeply AI enters daily life this way, the more naturally one arrives at a deeper question: when technology moves into the fabric of our lives, how does it change us—not only in terms of convenience, but in terms of health, relationships, and trust?

Physical AI and Human Life

That question is, for me, the most compelling one in AI research.

On one hand, there is much to look forward to. Through Physical AI or Home IoT, a system might sense the circumstances within a home and support the daily lives of those who need it—people living with depression, the elderly, or children in dual-income households. I am curious about the forms such technologies might take, and how they could help make people’s lives healthier and happier.

On the other hand, the same technology carries the potential to alter how people relate to one another—and in ways that are harder to see. Children today are already growing up alongside AI tablets and robots. When one robot’s service was discontinued, children’s grief over its loss became a topic of conversation; questions are increasingly being raised about how sustained interaction with AI devices may affect language development and sociality. Pixar’s Toy Story 5 captures something of this anxiety: through Lilypad, a frog-shaped smart tablet that becomes the young protagonist’s new favorite, the film quietly asks what screen-centered childhoods mean for play, friendship, and connection.

AI offers the answers people want, and offers them kindly. But conversation between people is never fully predictable—people react unexpectedly, say things we did not anticipate, and are emotionally complex. If people come to confide their most private thoughts in AI while slowly losing trust in one another, society could drift toward greater individualism and mutual distrust.

These questions do not undermine the promise of innovation—they are part of it. Understanding what technology truly brings to people’s lives, including its unintended effects, is itself a form of preparation for the future.

Innovation does not come merely from making well what is needed right now. It comes from imagining a future most people cannot yet see clearly, from believing in the technology that future will require, and from continuing to build it even when there seems to be no demand at all. I want to become a researcher who leads innovation in just that way—imagining the future, thinking through its impact, and working to understand what real change a technology brings to people’s lives.
