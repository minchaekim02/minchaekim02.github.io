Where Innovation Comes From: Reflections on Jensen Huang's Talk

"Innovation comes from a firm belief that something will happen five to ten years from now—that this technology will be needed."

I recently attended NVIDIA's Build-a-Claw special lecture. With the release of OpenClaw, NVIDIA's DGX Spark and the Mac mini have drawn renewed attention as personal computing environments for the next generation. Unlike conventional LLMs that respond to a single query at a time, OpenClaw enables an agent to carry out an entire task autonomously through channels like Slack and Telegram. Being open source, it can be configured freely—but running it continuously requires a dedicated personal machine that stays on at all times. This is precisely why the DGX Spark and the Mac mini have returned to the spotlight: they make possible an always-on local agent with direct access to files and information stored on the device.

Looking at Technology Through the Lens of the Future

What left the strongest impression was Jensen Huang's way of looking at technology—not measuring it against present demand, but against a future that had not yet arrived.

He began studying computers back when programming was just becoming possible, and in doing so saw countless possibilities. In an era when people were still navigating computers through Windows, he imagined what would come next and built NVIDIA technologies for which there was, at the time, no demand at all. The early DGX systems were much the same: little appetite existed when they first appeared, yet those systems—offered to research organizations such as OpenAI before the market was ready—went on to become the backbone of the AI research infrastructure that followed.

The infrastructure NVIDIA built has since become a foundation the entire AI ecosystem relies on—from CPUs and GPUs to AI agents and Physical AI.

As he put it:

"Innovation comes from a firm belief that something will happen five to ten years from now—that this technology will be needed."

And when an early attempt did not succeed, he did not give up—he immediately set about building something new and moved toward the next possibility.

Apple, Microsoft, Tesla, Meta—many of the companies that reshaped the world were built in exactly this way. Rather than responding to existing demand, they imagined changes most people had yet to sense and prepared for a future they believed in.

I, too, want to become someone who looks at the future that way—sensing problems before they come clearly into view, and preparing the research to address them.

Build-a-Claw, and the Promise of AI Agents

The Build-a-Claw demo, which had been the day's main purpose, took place after Jensen Huang's talk. We connected a DGX Spark to a computer and a phone, and as commands were issued through Telegram, we could watch several agents being orchestrated in real time. Going beyond answering a single question, the way the agents distributed and carried out an entire task on their own was clearly a different experience from how I had used LLMs before.

If an agent were to act on the files and information stored on my own computer, concerns about privacy would surely exist. At the same time, I found myself imagining a mode of computing—and a kind of innovation—unlike anything until now. And the more deeply AI enters our daily lives this way, the more naturally I was led to the next question: when technology moves into the fabric of our lives, how does it change us?

Physical AI and Human Life

What interests me most is exactly this: when AI enters our lives, how does it affect not only our convenience but our health, our relationships, and our trust?

On one hand, I look forward to the ways such technology can help people. Through Physical AI or Home IoT, a system might sense the various circumstances within a home and care for the daily lives of those who need support—people living with conditions such as depression, the elderly, or children in dual-income households. I am curious about the forms these technologies might take in the future, and how they could make people's lives healthier and happier.

On the other hand, the same technology carries the potential to alter how people relate to one another. More specifically, it may gradually reshape human relationships in ways that are harder to see. Children today are already growing up alongside AI tablets and robots. When one robot's service was discontinued, the sight of children grieving became a topic of conversation; cases are increasingly emerging about how interaction with AI devices may affect children's language development and sociality. The recently released Toy Story 5 confronts this directly—with the arrival of the smart tablet "LilyPad," the young protagonist gradually loses the time she once spent with her toys and her friends, showing how digital devices are remaking play and connection for the youngest generation.

AI offers the answers people want, and offers them kindly. But conversation between people is never fully predictable—people react unexpectedly, say things we did not anticipate, and are emotionally complex. If people come to confide their most private thoughts in AI while slowly losing trust in one another, society could drift toward greater individualism and mutual distrust.

So while technology can make people healthier and happier, we must keep asking how it also reshapes human relationships and trust.

Through this lecture and demo, I came to think, once again, about a single idea. Innovation does not come merely from making well what is needed right now. It comes from imagining a future most people cannot yet see clearly, from believing in the technology that future will surely require, and from continuing to build it even in the moments when there seems to be no demand at all.

I want to become a researcher who leads innovation in just that way—imagining the future, thinking through its impact, and working to understand what real change a technology brings to people's lives, so that I might draw a little closer to the future I believe in.
