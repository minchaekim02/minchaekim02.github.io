Where Innovation Comes From: Reflections on Jensen Huang's Talk

"Innovation comes from a firm belief that something will happen five to ten years from now—that this technology will be needed."

I recently attended NVIDIA's Build-a-Claw special lecture. With the release of OpenClaw, NVIDIA's DGX Spark and the Mac mini have drawn renewed attention as the personal computing environment of the next generation. Unlike conventional LLMs that respond to a single query at a time, OpenClaw allows an agent to carry out an entire task on its own through channels like Slack and Telegram. Because it is open source, you configure it to your own liking before running it—but to use it freely from anywhere, you need a personal machine that can stay running at all times. That is precisely why the DGX Spark and the Mac mini have returned to the spotlight: they make possible an agent that performs the tasks you want while holding all of the files and information on your own computer.

Looking at Technology Through the Lens of the Future

When Jensen Huang took the stage, he described Korea as a country reaching the top tier across so many fields—music, culture, technology—and said he holds high expectations for its future in AI and robotics as well. He even remarked that anything gets better once you add a "K" to it, which earned him the nickname "K-Jensen" for the day.

What struck me about him was not simply that he is a successful person. It was his way of looking at technology—measuring it not against the demand of the present, but against a future that has not yet arrived.

He began studying computers back when programming was just becoming possible, and in doing so he saw countless possibilities. In an era when people were still using computers through Windows, he imagined the situations that would follow and built NVIDIA technologies for which, at the time, there was no demand at all. The early DGX systems were much the same: there was little appetite for them when they first appeared, but those systems—offered to research organizations such as OpenAI when demand was still scarce—went on to connect with the AI research infrastructure that followed.

Today, in the age of AI, the technologies NVIDIA has built out have become a foundation that everyone relies on. CPUs and GPUs go without saying, but NVIDIA now accompanies so many moments of new innovation as well—AI agents, humanoids, Physical AI.

"Innovation comes from a firm belief that something will happen five to ten years from now—that this technology will be needed."

And when an early attempt did not succeed, he said, he did not give up; he immediately set about building something new and moved toward the next possibility.

The world has no shortage of innovators—people who imagine a future others cannot yet picture, and who prepare, in advance, the technologies and stories that future will require. Apple, Microsoft, Tesla, Meta, and countless other companies were founded in just this way. Rather than looking only at present demand, they imagined and prepared for changes and new tools that most people had yet to feel coming.

I, too, want to become someone who looks at the future in that way—someone who senses problems before they come clearly into view, and who prepares the technologies and the research to solve them.

Build-a-Claw, and the Promise of AI Agents

The Build-a-Claw demo, which had been the day's main purpose, took place after Jensen Huang's talk. We connected a DGX Spark to a computer and a phone, and as commands were issued through Telegram, we could watch several agents being orchestrated in real time. Going beyond answering a single question, the way the agents distributed and carried out an entire task on their own was clearly a different experience from how I had used LLMs before.

If an agent were to act on the files and information stored on my own computer, concerns about privacy would surely exist. At the same time, I found myself imagining a mode of computing—and a kind of innovation—unlike anything until now. And the more deeply AI enters our daily lives this way, the more naturally I was led to the next question: when technology moves into the fabric of our lives, how does it change us?

Physical AI and Human Life

What interests me most is exactly this: when AI enters our lives, how does it affect not only our convenience but our health, our relationships, and our trust?

On one hand, I look forward to the ways such technology can help people. Through Physical AI or Home IoT, a system might sense the various circumstances within a home and care for the daily lives of those who need support—people living with conditions such as depression, the elderly, or children in dual-income households. I am curious about the forms these technologies might take in the future, and how they could make people's lives healthier and happier.

On the other hand, I also see how the same technology could reshape human relationships. Children today are already growing up in environments where they are raised and taught alongside AI tablets and robots. When one robot's service was discontinued, the sight of children grieving became a topic of conversation, and cases are increasingly emerging about how interaction with AI-based devices may affect children's language and sociality. The recently released Toy Story 5 confronts this very shift. With the arrival of the smart tablet "LilyPad," the young protagonist gradually loses the time she once spent with her toys—and with her friends—showing how digital devices are remaking play and relationships for the youngest generation.

AI offers users the answers they want, and offers them kindly. But conversation between people is never entirely predictable. People react unexpectedly, say things we did not anticipate, and are emotionally complex beings. If people come to confide their most private information in AI while slowly losing their trust in one another, society could drift toward greater individualism and mutual distrust.

So while I believe technology can make people healthier and happier, I also think we must keep asking how that same technology reshapes human relationships and trust.

Through this lecture and demo, I came to think, once again, about a single idea. Innovation does not come merely from making well what is needed right now. It comes from imagining a future most people cannot yet see clearly, from believing in the technology that future will surely require, and from continuing to build it even in the moments when there seems to be no demand at all.

I want to become a researcher who leads innovation in just that way—imagining the future, thinking through its impact, and working to understand what real change a technology brings to people's lives, so that I might draw a little closer to the future I believe in.
